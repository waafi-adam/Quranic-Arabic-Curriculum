# Phase 2: The Four Properties of the Ism

> "Every Ism in Arabic carries four pieces of information with it at all times. If you can identify these four properties, you can decode the grammar of almost any Quranic ayah."

**Prerequisite:** You must be able to identify an Ism (Phase 1) before starting this phase.

---

## Property 1: Status (الإعراب / I'rab)

### The Big Idea

Status answers the question: **What job is this Ism doing in the sentence?**

Arabic communicates the role of a word through its **ending sound**. English uses word order ("The dog bit the man" vs. "The man bit the dog"). Arabic uses endings — which means Arabic word order is flexible, but the endings are critical.

There are **three statuses**:

### Raf' (الرفع) — The Doer Status

The Ism is the **subject** or the **main topic** of the sentence.

| Ending Type | Raf' Ending | Example |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| With tanween | -un (ٌ) | مُسْلِمٌ (a Muslim) |
| With "Al" | -u (ُ) | الْمُسْلِمُ (the Muslim) |

> **Quranic Example:**
> جَاءَ **الْحَقُّ** — "**The truth** came."
>
> الْحَقُّ ends in damma (ُ) = Raf' status = it is the doer of the verb "came."

### Nasb (النصب) — The Detail Status

The Ism provides **extra detail** — it's the object, or tells you when, where, why, or how.

| Ending Type | Nasb Ending | Example |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| With tanween | -an (ً) | مُسْلِمًا (a Muslim) |
| With "Al" | -a (َ) | الْمُسْلِمَ (the Muslim) |

> **Quranic Example:**
> إِنَّ **اللَّهَ** غَفُورٌ — "Certainly **Allah** is forgiving."
>
> اللَّهَ ends in fat-ha (َ) = Nasb status. Why? Because إِنَّ forces the next Ism into Nasb.

### Jarr (الجر) — The "Of/After Preposition" Status

The Ism comes after a Harf of Jarr or is the second word of an Idhafah (possessive).

| Ending Type | Jarr Ending | Example |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| With tanween | -in (ٍ) | مُسْلِمٍ (a Muslim) |
| With "Al" | -i (ِ) | الْمُسْلِمِ (the Muslim) |

> **Quranic Example:**
> بِسْمِ **اللَّهِ** — "In the name **of Allah**."
>
> اللَّهِ ends in kasra (ِ) = Jarr status. Why? It is part of an Idhafah (اسم + الله = name of Allah).

### The Status Chart (Memorize This)

| Status | Role | With ال | With Tanween |
|--------|------|---------|--------------|
| **Raf'** | Doer/Subject | -u (ُ) | -un (ٌ) |
| **Nasb** | Detail/Object | -a (َ) | -an (ً) |
| **Jarr** | After "of"/preposition | -i (ِ) | -in (ٍ) |

### Light vs. Heavy Isms

Not all Isms follow the chart above. Some are **light** — they don't take tanween and have slightly different endings. You'll learn why in the discussion of **flexibility** later. For now, just know that there are two categories:

| Type | Raf' | Nasb | Jarr | Example |
|------|------|------|------|---------|
| **Fully Flexible** | -u / -un | -a / -an | -i / -in | كِتَابٌ / كِتَابًا / كِتَابٍ |
| **Partly Flexible** | -u | -a | -a (!) | أَحْمَدُ / أَحْمَدَ / أَحْمَدَ |

Partly flexible Isms (like most proper names and some plural patterns) use fat-ha for **both** Nasb and Jarr. They also never take tanween.

> **Quranic Example of Partly Flexible:**
> مَسَاجِدَ اللَّهِ — "The masajid (mosques) of Allah."
>
> مَسَاجِدَ is a broken plural. Its Jarr ending is fat-ha (َ) instead of kasra, because it is partly flexible.

## Property 2: Number (العدد)

Arabic doesn't just have singular and plural — it has **three** numbers:

### Singular (مفرد / Mufrad)
One person or thing.
- مُسْلِمٌ — one Muslim (male)
- مُسْلِمَةٌ — one Muslim (female)

### Dual (مثنى / Muthanna)
Exactly two. English doesn't have this — Arabic does, and the Quran uses it frequently.
- مُسْلِمَانِ — two Muslims (male, in Raf')
- مُسْلِمَيْنِ — two Muslims (male, in Nasb/Jarr)
- مُسْلِمَتَانِ — two Muslims (female, in Raf')
- مُسْلِمَتَيْنِ — two Muslims (female, in Nasb/Jarr)

**Key Rule:** Dual endings change based on status:
- Raf' = انِ (-aani)
- Nasb/Jarr = يْنِ (-ayni)

> **Quranic Example:**
> رَبُّ الْمَشْرِقَيْنِ وَرَبُّ الْمَغْرِبَيْنِ (Ar-Rahman 55:17)
>
> "Lord of the two Easts and Lord of the two Wests."
> الْمَشْرِقَيْنِ — dual, Jarr (after Idhafah), ending يْنِ.

### Plural (جمع / Jam')

Arabic has **three types** of plurals:

#### 1. Sound Masculine Plural (جمع مذكر سالم)
Add ونَ or ينَ to the end.

| Status | Ending | Example |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Raf' | -oona (ونَ) | مُسْلِمُونَ |
| Nasb/Jarr | -eena (ينَ) | مُسْلِمِينَ |

> **Quranic Example:**
> وَالصَّابِرِينَ — "And the patient ones." (Nasb — ينَ ending)

#### 2. Sound Feminine Plural (جمع مؤنث سالم)
Replace the ة with ات.

| Status | Ending | Example |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Raf' | -aatun (اتٌ) | مُسْلِمَاتٌ |
| Nasb/Jarr | -aatin (اتٍ) | مُسْلِمَاتٍ |

**Special note:** The Sound Feminine Plural uses kasra for **both** Nasb and Jarr (the opposite of the partly flexible pattern).

#### 3. Broken Plural (جمع تكسير)
The internal structure of the word changes. There is no single rule — these are memorized through patterns.

| Singular | Broken Plural | Pattern |
|----------|--------------|---------|
| كِتَاب (book) | كُتُب (books) | فُعُل |
| رَجُل (man) | رِجَال (men) | فِعَال |
| مَسْجِد (mosque) | مَسَاجِد (mosques) | مَفَاعِل |

Broken plurals are treated as **singular feminine** for grammar purposes (this is important for verb agreement).

## Property 3: Gender (الجنس)

Arabic has two genders: **masculine** (مذكر) and **feminine** (مؤنث).

### How to Identify Feminine Words

| Sign | Example | Meaning |
|------|---------|---------|
| Ends in ة (taa marbuta) | مَدْرَسَة | School |
| Ends in اء (alif + hamza) | صَحْرَاء | Desert |
| Ends in ى (alif maqsura) | كُبْرَى | Greatest (fem.) |

### "Fake" Feminine (مؤنث غير حقيقي)

Some words are grammatically feminine even though they don't refer to females and may not have a feminine ending:

| Word | Meaning | Why Feminine? |
|------|---------|---------------|
| شَمْس | Sun | Convention |
| أَرْض | Earth/Land | Convention |
| نَار | Fire | Convention |
| رِيح | Wind | Convention |
| نَفْس | Soul/Self | Convention |

These words must be treated as feminine in all grammar rules (verb agreement, adjective matching, etc.).

> **Quranic Example:**
> إِذَا الشَّمْسُ كُوِّرَتْ (At-Takwir 81:1)
>
> "When the sun is wrapped up."
> The verb كُوِّرَتْ has a تْ at the end — this is the **feminine verb marker**, because شَمْس is feminine.

### Why Gender Matters

Gender affects:
1. Which verb form to use (masculine vs. feminine)
2. Which adjective form to use
3. Which pronoun to use
4. Which demonstrative pronoun to use (هٰذَا vs. هٰذِهِ)

## Property 4: Type (النوع)

Is the Ism referring to something **specific** (definite) or **general** (indefinite)?

### Definite (معرفة / Ma'rifa)

A word is definite if it refers to something **specific and known**. There are several ways a word becomes definite:

| Method | Example | Meaning |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Has ال | الكِتَاب | **The** book |
| Is a proper name | مُحَمَّد | Muhammad |
| Has a pronoun attached | كِتَابُهُ | **His** book |
| Is the first word of Idhafah (pointing to something definite) | كِتَابُ اللهِ | Book **of Allah** (= definite) |

### Indefinite (نكرة / Nakira)

A word is indefinite if it refers to something **general and unspecified**. The sign of an indefinite Ism is **tanween**.

| Example | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| كِتَابٌ | **A** book |
| رَجُلٌ | **A** man |
| مَدِينَةٍ | **A** city |

### Why Type Matters

1. **Adjectives must match.** A definite noun needs a definite adjective:
   - الكِتَابُ الكَبِيرُ — "The big book" (both have ال)
   - كِتَابٌ كَبِيرٌ — "A big book" (both have tanween)

2. **Idhafah rules.** The first word of Idhafah **loses** its ال and tanween.

3. **Sentence structure.** A definite Ism at the beginning of a sentence is usually the subject (Mubtada) of a Jumla Ismiyya.

---

## Putting It All Together: The 4-Property Analysis

For any Ism in the Quran, you should be able to identify all four properties:

> **Example:** الْمُسْلِمِينَ
>
> 1. **Status:** Jarr (or Nasb) — ending is ينَ
> 2. **Number:** Plural — Sound Masculine Plural
> 3. **Gender:** Masculine
> 4. **Type:** Definite — has ال
>
> **Translation clue:** "the Muslims" (in a Jarr or Nasb context)

> **Example:** رَسُولًا
>
> 1. **Status:** Nasb — ending is ً (tanween with fat-ha)
> 2. **Number:** Singular
> 3. **Gender:** Masculine
> 4. **Type:** Indefinite — has tanween
>
> **Translation clue:** "a messenger" (as an object or detail)

## Key Takeaway

> An Ism is never just a word — it always carries its Status, Number, Gender, and Type. These four properties are like a word's ID card. Master them, and the grammar of the Quran opens up.
